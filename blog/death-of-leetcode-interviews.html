The Death of LeetCode Interviews: What Comes Next

January 5, 2025 7 min read

For over a decade, LeetCode-style algorithm interviews have been the gatekeepers of tech hiring. But with AI able to solve most of these problems instantly, it's time to ask: what were we actually measuring?

The Original Promise

Algorithm interviews emerged for good reasons. Early tech companies needed to filter thousands of applicants quickly. Standardized problems allowed consistent evaluation. And algorithmic thinking does correlate with engineering ability - to a point.

The problem is that correlation became causation in hiring teams' minds. "Good at LeetCode" became synonymous with "good engineer."

What LeetCode Actually Measures

Let's be honest about what these interviews test:

  • Pattern recognition: Have you seen this problem type before?
  • Memorization: Do you remember the standard approaches?
  • Performance under pressure: Can you think while someone watches?
  • Preparation time: How many hours did you study?

Notice what's missing: debugging, system design, code review, collaboration, communication, and judgment. The skills that actually determine whether someone ships reliable software.

The AI Disruption

AI didn't just make LeetCode problems easier to solve - it exposed their fundamental weakness. If a problem can be solved by describing it to an AI, the solution wasn't the valuable part.

What AI can't do (yet) is understand messy requirements, debug distributed systems, make architectural trade-offs, or communicate effectively with stakeholders. These are the skills that matter.

What Should Replace LeetCode?

The next generation of technical interviews should evaluate:

  • Problem decomposition: Can candidates break ambiguous requirements into clear tasks?
  • Verification skills: Do they test their assumptions? Catch edge cases?
  • AI collaboration: Can they effectively guide and verify AI-generated code?
  • Debugging: Can they trace issues through complex systems using logs and traces?
  • Communication: Can they explain their thinking clearly?

The Resistance

Change is slow in hiring. LeetCode interviews persist because they're easy to administer, easy to compare, and "that's how we've always done it."

But companies paying the price - in bad hires, missed talent, and engineering hours wasted on interview prep - are starting to experiment with alternatives.

Making the Transition

If you're a hiring manager considering the switch, start small:

  • Add a debugging challenge alongside your algorithm round
  • Let candidates use AI assistants and evaluate their collaboration
  • Include a system design discussion focused on trade-offs, not optimal solutions
  • Track which interview signals actually predict on-the-job performance

The Future

In five years, asking candidates to implement quicksort on a whiteboard will seem as outdated as asking them to write machine code. The engineers who thrive will be those who can leverage AI effectively while maintaining the judgment to build reliable systems.

The question isn't whether LeetCode interviews will die - it's whether your company will adapt before or after your competitors.
