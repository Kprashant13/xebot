How to Evaluate AI Collaboration Skills in Interviews

December 28, 2024 9 min read

Working with AI is a skill. Some engineers use AI effectively to multiply their output; others struggle or produce unreliable code. Here's how to evaluate this increasingly critical capability.

Why This Matters

AI coding assistants are becoming standard tools. Engineers who use them well ship faster and with fewer bugs. Those who don't either avoid them entirely (leaving productivity on the table) or use them poorly (introducing subtle bugs and technical debt).

Hiring for AI collaboration skills is no longer optional - it's how you build competitive engineering teams.

What Good AI Collaboration Looks Like

Effective AI collaborators share several characteristics:

  • Clear prompting: They communicate requirements precisely, knowing that ambiguity leads to wrong solutions
  • Iterative refinement: They guide AI through multiple rounds, building on partial solutions
  • Critical evaluation: They don't blindly accept AI output - they verify, test, and question
  • Strategic usage: They know when to use AI and when to write code directly
  • Error recovery: They recognize AI mistakes and course-correct efficiently

Interview Structure

An effective AI collaboration assessment has three phases:

Phase 1: Problem Setup (5 minutes)

Give the candidate a realistic problem with some ambiguity. Good problems require clarification before implementation - this tests whether candidates ask good questions or rush to prompting.

Phase 2: AI-Assisted Implementation (30-40 minutes)

Let the candidate work with their preferred AI tool. Observe:

  • How do they structure their prompts?
  • Do they break the problem into pieces or try to solve it all at once?
  • How do they respond when AI gives a wrong or incomplete answer?
  • Do they verify the solution? How?
  • Can they explain what the AI-generated code does?

Phase 3: Discussion (10-15 minutes)

Ask the candidate to explain their approach. Good questions include:

  • "Why did you structure your prompts that way?"
  • "How did you verify the AI's solution was correct?"
  • "What would you do differently if you were building this for production?"
  • "Where might this solution have edge case bugs?"

Red Flags to Watch For

Certain behaviors indicate poor AI collaboration skills:

  • Copy-paste without reading: Taking AI output verbatim without review
  • Prompt repetition: Asking the same question multiple times expecting different results
  • Frustration spirals: Getting stuck when AI doesn't immediately produce the right answer
  • Over-prompting: Writing longer and longer prompts instead of breaking problems down
  • No testing: Assuming AI code works without verification
  • Can't explain: Unable to describe what the generated code does

Green Flags to Celebrate

Signs of strong AI collaboration:

  • Problem decomposition: Breaking complex problems into AI-friendly chunks
  • Iterative dialogue: Building solutions through back-and-forth refinement
  • Active verification: Testing edge cases and questioning assumptions
  • Confident editing: Modifying AI output based on their own judgment
  • Strategic bypassing: Writing code directly when that's more efficient

Scoring Framework

We recommend evaluating on these dimensions:

  • Prompt quality (25%): Clear, specific, well-structured prompts
  • Verification behavior (25%): Testing, edge case consideration, code review
  • Iteration efficiency (20%): How quickly they converge on a solution
  • Code understanding (20%): Ability to explain and modify AI output
  • Strategic judgment (10%): Knowing when to use AI vs. code directly

Common Objections

"Won't candidates just game this?"

The interview tests real skills. Unlike memorizing algorithm solutions, you can't fake effective AI collaboration. And if someone can genuinely collaborate well with AI, that's exactly what you want.

"What if candidates aren't familiar with our AI tool?"

Let them use their preferred tool. The skills transfer across AI assistants. If they're effective with Claude, they'll be effective with GPT or Copilot.

"Doesn't this disadvantage people who've had less AI exposure?"

Somewhat, but AI collaboration is a learnable skill. Someone with strong fundamentals who's new to AI can pick it up quickly. The interview reveals their potential, not just their current experience.

Getting Started

If you're adding AI collaboration to your interview process:

  • Start with one round, adding it alongside existing interviews
  • Train interviewers on what to observe
  • Calibrate by having engineers of different levels take the interview
  • Iterate on problems based on what reveals signal

The companies that figure out AI collaboration hiring now will have a significant advantage in building their teams over the next few years.
