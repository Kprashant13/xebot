Debugging as a Core Engineering Skill: Why It Matters More Than Ever

December 22, 2024 7 min read

There's a common misconception that AI will make debugging obsolete. The reality is the opposite: as AI generates more code, the ability to debug becomes the essential human skill.

The Debugging Paradox

AI can write code quickly, but it can't reliably debug its own output. It doesn't have access to your production environment. It can't observe the actual behavior of running systems. It can't connect the dots between a customer complaint and a race condition in your cache layer.

This means the more code AI generates, the more debugging humans need to do.

What Debugging Really Is

Debugging isn't just "finding bugs." It's a systematic process of:

  • Observation: What is the system actually doing vs. what should it do?
  • Hypothesis formation: What could explain this behavior?
  • Experimentation: How can I test my hypothesis?
  • Analysis: What does the evidence tell me?
  • Resolution: How do I fix it without breaking something else?

This process requires judgment, experience, and intuition that AI currently lacks.

Why AI Can't Debug (Yet)

Several fundamental limitations prevent AI from effective debugging:

  • No production access: AI can't see your logs, metrics, or traces
  • No system context: It doesn't know your architecture, dependencies, or history
  • No user context: It can't talk to customers or understand business impact
  • No iteration: It can't run experiments and observe results
  • Hallucination risk: AI might confidently suggest fixes that don't make sense

The Debugging Skill Gap

Many engineers struggle with debugging because it's rarely taught formally. CS curricula focus on writing code, not investigating failures. Boot camps teach frameworks, not troubleshooting.

This creates a two-tier workforce: engineers who can only write new code, and engineers who can also maintain and fix existing systems. The second group is far more valuable.

Building Debugging Skills

Debugging is learnable. Here's how to develop the skill:

Study Systems

Understand how things work at a deep level. When you know how databases execute queries, network packets flow, and memory is allocated, you can form better hypotheses about failures.

Practice Deliberately

Don't just fix bugs - study them. After resolving an issue, ask: What was the root cause? How did it manifest? What was my debugging process? What would I do differently?

Learn Your Tools

Master debuggers, profilers, log aggregators, and tracing systems. The right tool can turn hours of investigation into minutes.

Read Postmortems

Other teams' failure analyses are goldmines. They expose failure patterns you might never encounter otherwise.

Debugging in Interviews

Traditional interviews don't test debugging. Writing binary search on a whiteboard doesn't reveal whether someone can trace a production issue through distributed services.

At Xebot, we give candidates real debugging scenarios: logs from a failing service, traces showing latency issues, metrics dashboards during an outage. We watch their process, not just their answer.

What Good Debugging Looks Like

Expert debuggers share certain behaviors:

  • They stay calm: Panic makes debugging worse
  • They gather data first: Understanding before action
  • They question assumptions: Including their own
  • They narrow systematically: Binary search for bugs
  • They document: So the next person learns too

The Future of Debugging

AI will eventually help with debugging - suggesting hypotheses, correlating logs, identifying patterns. But humans will remain in the loop, providing context, making judgment calls, and taking responsibility.

The engineers who thrive will be those who can partner with AI: using it to accelerate investigation while providing the insight and judgment that AI lacks.

Start Now

If you want to become a better debugger:

  • Volunteer for on-call rotations
  • Investigate issues outside your area
  • Study your observability tools deeply
  • Read every postmortem you can find
  • Practice explaining your debugging process out loud

Debugging is a superpower. In an AI-assisted world, it's the superpower.
